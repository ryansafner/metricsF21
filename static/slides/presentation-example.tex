\begin{frame}{Overview}
\protect\hypertarget{overview}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  As a student writing an empirical research paper, does writing a
  longer paper attain a higher grade?
\item
  Simple OLS regression
\item
  Sample data collected from previous classes with paper assignments
\item
  Strong positive effect: for every marginal page written, grades
  improve by about 1-2 points
\item
  Robust to different models
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Theory}
\protect\hypertarget{theory}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Dilemma:
\end{itemize}

\begin{enumerate}
\tightlist
\item
  Longer papers might imply students work hard and write a good paper
\item
  But students might also put in low quality filler hoping to inflate
  their grades
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Data}
\protect\hypertarget{data}{}
\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  I collected data from 7 different classes at 2 different colleges
  where I assigned a longer term paper
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Implications}
\protect\hypertarget{implications}{}
\begin{itemize}
\item
  For every additional page written, we can expect a paper's grade to
  increase by about a point or less.
\item
  For econometrics only, marginal effect is even smaller, only less than
  half of a point increase for every additional page written.
\item
  Likely endogeneity of length due to unobserved factors such as topic
  and quality of writing
\item
  \emph{It would be poor advice to recommend students simply to write
  long papers to earn a higher grade.}
\end{itemize}
